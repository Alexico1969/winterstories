Winter Story
By You
Start
On a cold, snowy day in December, the sky hung low and heavy, smothered by layers of bluish-gray clouds. Snow drifted down in swirling sheets, soft at first, then sharper, faster, as if someone had turned a dial from dgentle flurries to a full-blown storm. Alex pulled his hood tighter around his face as he balanced on the steep roof of his friend’s house, trying his best to string up the last strand of Christmas lights before the incoming blizzard swallowed the daylight.
He had promised his friend—who was unexpectedly called out of town—that he would finish the decorations before they returned. Alex wasn’t the type to break a promise, but as he felt the icy wind cutting at his cheeks and saw how quickly the snow was piling up, he knew he was pushing his luck.
“Yeah… that’s enough for today,” he muttered, squinting through the thickening snowfall.
The roof suddenly jolted under his boots as a gust of wind slammed into the house. Alex’s foot slipped, and he dropped into a crouch, grabbing the edge of a shingle to steady himself. His heart hammered.
“Okay. Definitely enough.”
He scrambled down the ladder as fast as he safely could, dragging the tangled leftover lights with him. By the time his boots hit the ground, the storm had accelerated into a white curtain, and it was nearly impossible to see more than a couple of yards ahead.
He jogged toward his car, brushing off the thick layer of snow that had already gathered on the windshield. His fingers stung as he opened the driver’s door and slid inside, slamming it quickly to keep the cold out. Alex shivered and tried to catch his breath.
“Come on… please start,” he whispered, turning the key in the ignition.
The engine groaned. Coughed. And died.
He tried again. Nothing this time—not even a cough. Just silence and the drumming of snow on the roof of the car.
“No. No, no, no!” He smacked the steering wheel. “Not today.”
He waited a moment, turned the key again.
Still dead.
He realized with growing dread that the temperature had dropped so fast and so severely that the car's engine had completely frozen. He was stranded.
Alex stared through the windshield at the buried neighborhood. The storm grew thicker by the second. He didn’t even know if he could make it home on foot, and he wasn’t sure what frostbite felt like, but he didn’t want to find out.
His only chance was his friend’s house.
Alex grabbed his backpack, pulled his jacket tight again, and sprinted toward the front door. Snow soaked through his jeans almost instantly, the cold biting into his skin. He banged on the door, shouting his friend’s name.
No answer.
He tried again, pounding harder.
Still nothing.
Alex stepped back, snow now covering his hair and shoulders. He looked around, desperate. Maybe the house was just too insulated for anyone to hear him. Maybe someone was home but asleep. Or wearing headphones. Or in the basement.
But the cold was already seeping into his bones.
“I’m sorry,” he whispered to no one in particular.
He moved to the side of the house, found a window that looked older—one he knew didn’t lock properly because he and his friend had fixed it last year—and with one hard shove using his elbow and a nearby snow shovel, he cracked it open. Glass shattered, letting in a burst of frigid air.
Alex climbed inside, brushing glass off his jacket as he stepped onto the carpet. The house was dim but warm—shockingly warm, actually—and Christmassy scents lingered in the air, like cinnamon and wood smoke.
But the first thing he noticed wasn’t the warmth.
It was the McDonald's bag sitting on the kitchen counter.
Fresh.
Unopened.
Steam drifting faintly from the top.
“What the…?” Alex muttered.
He stepped closer and pressed his fingers to the outside of the bag. Warm. Freshly warm—like it had been brought in minutes ago. But he had checked the driveway. There had been no cars. The house was silent. He hadn’t seen or heard anyone.
Still, hunger outweighed suspicion. He hadn’t eaten since morning, and his stomach growled loudly in the quiet house. He opened the bag and pulled out a burger and fries. Everything tasted like it was just bought.
But with every bite, a strange unease crept up his spine.
Something was off.
He wiped his mouth and walked through the house cautiously, checking the living room, the hallway, the guest rooms. All empty. The heating hummed softly through the vents. Nothing was out of place. Nothing seemed disturbed.
Just him, the storm, and the eerie warmth of an empty home that shouldn’t have warm, fresh food sitting out.
Alex stepped toward the back of the house, listening carefully.
A distant noise—like crunching snow—came from outside.
He froze.
Then another sound: tires slowly moving over gravel.
A car.
Someone was pulling into the driveway.
Panic jolted through him. If his friend wasn’t supposed to be home, then who was this? And worse—what would they think about a broken window and a stranger in their kitchen eating their food?
Alex’s mind raced.
He had two choices:
Hide.
Or…
Meet whoever was coming in head-on and try to explain.
His breath hitched. Footsteps crunched outside. A car door slammed shut.
Alex had seconds left to decide.
Middle
Alex’s instincts pulled him in two directions. Part of him wanted to hide—duck under the table, slip into a closet, something. Anything. Maybe he could avoid a confrontation until he figured out who this mystery person was and why they were here.
But the other part of him knew that hiding in someone else’s house, especially after breaking a window, would make him look like a criminal.
“Okay… okay, calm down,” he whispered to himself.
Another set of footsteps approached the house—slow, heavy, certain.
Alex swallowed and made his choice.
He stepped toward the front door.
Snow-laden air blasted in as the door swung open. A tall figure stepped inside, brushing snow from their jacket. Alex barely had time to speak before the person looked up and froze.
It wasn’t his friend.
It was someone Alex had never seen before.
A man in his late forties with a thick beard, sharp eyes, and an expression that flickered between confusion and suspicion.
“Who the hell are you?” the man demanded.
Alex raised his hands slightly—not in surrender, but in a plea for calm. “I—I can explain. My car wouldn’t start. The storm got bad. I knocked but no one answered. I thought the house was empty.”
The man’s eyes narrowed.
“And you just decided to break in?”
“I didn’t want to,” Alex insisted. “But I would’ve frozen out there.”
The man set down the grocery bag he was holding—McDonald’s, just like the one on the counter.
Alex’s stomach dropped.
That meant the warm food hadn’t been left unattended.
It meant this man had returned only moments before Alex arrived.
“Look,” Alex said quickly, “I know how this looks. But I swear I’m not here to steal anything. I just needed shelter.”
The man studied him for a long, tense moment.
Then he sighed.
“Storm caught you too, huh?”
Alex blinked. “…Yeah.”
“Your friend,” the man said, “the person who owns this place—wasn’t home. I’m house-sitting while they’re away. They mentioned someone might come by to finish the lights, but I wasn’t expecting anyone today.”
Alex let out a shaky breath of relief. “So you’re not… calling the cops?”
“If you’d trashed the place or taken anything,” the man said, shrugging out of his jacket, “maybe. But you look more frozen and terrified than dangerous.”
Alex nearly collapsed from the tension draining out of his body.
The man brought him a blanket, gestured toward the fireplace, and tossed him one of the still-warm burgers. Alex sat down, thawing out, grateful beyond words. The house-sitter, who introduced himself as Daniel, shared how he had just gone out to pick up food but got delayed by the worsening storm.
Outside, the wind howled against the windows, rattling the broken one Alex had climbed through.
“We should cover that,” Daniel said. “Don’t want snow piling in.”
Together, they boarded it with a spare piece of plywood from the basement. When they were done, Alex apologized again for breaking it, offering to pay for repairs. Daniel waved him off, saying the storm was the real culprit.
As hours passed, the blizzard only intensified. Power flickered once, twice, and then finally blinked out completely. The house went dark except for the dim glow of the fireplace.
Ending
By nightfall, the storm was raging at full force, covering the world outside in endless white. Alex and Daniel sat near the fire, wrapped in blankets, sharing stories to pass the time. For two people who had started the day as total strangers—one breaking in, the other arriving unexpectedly—they quickly settled into easy conversation, united by circumstance and the shared challenge of surviving the winter’s fury.
Snow piled so high outside the door that the porch disappeared completely. The driveway vanished under feet of new snow. Neither of them would be going anywhere until morning, maybe longer.
Hours later, as the fire burned low and the house grew colder without heat, Alex stared into the flickering flames.
“I thought I was going to freeze out there,” he admitted quietly.
Daniel nodded. “Storms like this… they test people. I’m glad you came inside when you did.”
Alex smiled faintly. “Thanks for… not freaking out.”
“Well,” Daniel chuckled, “I might’ve freaked out a little.”
They both laughed.
When morning finally arrived, the storm had passed, leaving behind a world transformed—quiet, glittering, still. The power returned with a low hum. The sun broke through the clouds, making the snow sparkle like a million tiny diamonds.
Daniel helped Alex dig out his car, and after a jump-start and some coaxing, the engine sputtered back to life. Before Alex left, the two exchanged numbers—just in case either of them ever needed help again.
And as Alex drove slowly down the glistening road, he realized that the terrifying night had ended with something unexpected:
A reminder that even in the coldest storms, there was warmth to be found—in kindness, in connection, and in the simple act of helping a stranger.
And sometimes, a broken window was the beginning of something surprisingly good.
