The snow had fallen all night—thick, heavy flakes that blanketed the suburban New York neighborhood in a soft, pristine white. When twelve-year-old Daniel woke to the muffled silence of a snow day, he knew exactly how he wanted to spend it. More importantly, he knew his seven-year-old brother, Leo, would already be waiting at the door in his puffy blue coat, bouncing with excitement.
Sure enough, when Daniel trudged downstairs, Leo was standing on tiptoe trying to force his gloves on the wrong hands.
“You ready?” Daniel asked, grabbing his sled.
Leo grinned. “I’ve been ready since forever ago.”
Outside, the morning felt enchanted. Every roof shimmered beneath a sugar crust of frost, and their yard looked like a blank page waiting to be written on. The air smelled clean, sharp, and brand new.
They started with sledding. The hill at the end of their street wasn’t much, but today it felt like a mountain. Daniel pushed off first, Leo clutching his waist, both of them shrieking as the sled skidded down the slope and sent glittering snow spraying into the air.
“Again!” Leo squealed before they even stopped sliding.
They went again and again until their cheeks were pink and their numb fingers tingled inside their gloves. After that, they built a snowman—more lumpy than round, and a little crooked. Leo insisted on using blueberries for the eyes, though they kept falling out.
As they spilled back into the yard, Daniel felt a rare kind of peace. Snow days were escapes—little breaks from the world, sealed behind walls of drifting white. He wished this one would last forever.
But then things began to shift.
It was subtle at first. A gust of wind that felt too cold. A branch that cracked somewhere in the wooded area behind their house—a crack that echoed longer than it should have.
“Let’s make snow angels,” Leo said, already flopping into the snow.
Daniel smiled and dropped beside him.
They moved their arms and legs in sweeping motions, and the snow hissed around them. But as Daniel stood and brushed himself off, something in the woods caught his eye—movement. Not wildlife movement, either. Something tall. Too tall.
He blinked. Nothing.
Maybe just a shadow.
“Daniel, help me up!” Leo called.
Daniel reached down to grab him.
But the moment his fingers brushed Leo’s mitten, a sound drifted through the trees—a long, low howl that didn’t sound like any dog or coyote Daniel had ever heard. It was almost human, but stretched, warped, as if something were imitating a person and getting it wrong.
Leo froze. “What was that?”
Daniel forced a laugh, though his skin prickled. “Probably someone’s dog.”
But his voice trembled, and Leo heard it.
Still, they kept playing—because snow days were safe, weren’t they? Snow days were warm coats and frosty breath and harmless fun.
Except warmth and safety can melt quickly.
It happened in a moment. Daniel turned toward the porch to grab the carrot they’d forgotten for the snowman’s nose.
“Stay right there,” he told Leo. “Two seconds.”
It really was two seconds. Maybe three.
But when Daniel turned back, Leo wasn’t there.
The snow angel was there—Leo-shaped, mitten prints still fresh. But Leo himself was gone. The yard was empty, silent, and now the silence felt wrong.
“Leo?” Daniel called. He tried to sound playful. “Okay, seriously, not funny.”
No answer.
He scanned the yard, the street, the hill. The world was a white maze, every house nearly identical under the snow. Footprints crisscrossed the yard—but which were his? Which were Leo’s? Which were… someone else’s?
The wind picked up, carrying another long, stretched howl.
Daniel’s stomach dropped.
He followed the nearest set of small prints, heart pounding. They didn’t lead to the porch or the sled track. They led toward the woods.
“Leo!” he shouted, his voice cracking.
No answer—only the creak of branches, groaning under the weight of the snow.
Daniel stepped into the woods.
Shadows carved themselves into strange shapes between the trees. The air grew colder—the kind of cold that crawled under his coat and wrapped icy fingers around his bones. Wind swirled through, whispering something that sounded almost like words.
Daaaaniell…
He whipped around. “Who’s there?”
But everything was still again.
The footprints continued deeper, weaving between trees. Daniel followed, scanning desperately.
Then he saw it—a flash of blue.
Leo’s coat.
His heart leapt, and he sprinted toward it.
But when he reached the spot, the coat was hanging from a branch—as if placed there. Carefully. Deliberately.
And beneath the branch, the snow was… disturbed. Spiraled. As if someone—or something—had circled the area again and again.
Daniel shivered and gripped the coat.
“Leo,” he whispered, voice breaking.
And then—a sound. Soft. Wet. Sniffling.
He turned sharply.
“Leo?!”
A small shape peeked from behind a tree, trembling.
Daniel ran.
Leo burst into tears as Daniel threw his arms around him.
“I—I tried to follow you,” Leo sobbed. “But the snow felt weird and I got scared and then I heard something whispering my name.”
Daniel hugged him tighter. “It’s okay. I’m here. I’ve got you.”
A crack snapped somewhere behind them, but Daniel didn’t look back. He lifted Leo’s coat from the branch, shoved it around his brother’s shoulders, and guided him quickly out of the woods.
The moment they stepped back into their yard, the wind died. The shadows seemed to shrink. Whatever had lurked among the trees stayed behind, watching—or waiting.
Daniel didn’t stop until they were inside, door locked, boots kicked off in a messy pile.
Their mom rushed in, worried at the sight of Leo’s tear-streaked face, but Daniel only muttered, “He got scared. Lost for a minute. He’s fine now.”
When they were wrapped in blankets on the couch, Leo whispered, “Daniel… do you think something was in the woods?”
Daniel stared into his mug of hot chocolate, marshmallows melting into creamy swirls. “I think,” he said slowly, “the woods are really big. And snow makes everything sound strange.”
Leo leaned against him. “But you found me.”
“Of course I did,” Daniel said, ruffling his hair. “I always will.”
Outside, the snow continued falling softly, peacefully.
But at the edge of the woods, beyond what the boys could see, something tall and shadowy stood between the trees.
Watching.
Waiting.
Buried beneath the gentle hush of the snowfall, a faint sound drifted through the air—almost a whisper, long and stretched and wrong.
Leeeoooo…
The boys didn’t hear it. Not tonight.
Tonight, all they heard was the quiet crackle of the heater, the soft sip of hot chocolate, and the comforting sound of being safely, finally, home.
